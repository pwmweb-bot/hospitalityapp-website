Fridge temperature log, properly.

The rules

UK fridge temperature standards.

Food law (Regulation 853/2004 retained, plus the Food Safety and Hygiene (England) Regulations 2013 and equivalent in Wales, Scotland and NI) sets the maximum chilled-food storage temperature at 8°C. Best-practice operational targets sit well below that.

Legal maximum (chilled)

8°C — the statutory limit for chilled food storage. Above this is a food-safety offence.

Operational target (chilled)

0–5°C — the band most kitchens log against. Gives a 3°C buffer before any legal issue.

Freezer target

-18°C or colder — for frozen storage. Above -15°C should trigger corrective action.

Hot-hold minimum

63°C or above — for any hot-held cooked food. Below this and time-temperature controls apply.

The frequency

How often you actually need to log.

"Daily" satisfies the FSA's Safer Food Better Business pack. Twice daily satisfies most EHOs. Three times a day satisfies the inspector who's seen the kitchen before.

Opening check (every day).

First thing in. Reads taken before any door-opening that might've affected the cabinet. Catches overnight failures.

Mid-service check (busy kitchens).

Mid-lunch or mid-dinner. Catches the fridge that's struggling because the door's been open too long during prep.

Closing check (every day).

Last thing out. Confirms the cabinet's been steady all day; gives you the last reading before any overnight risk.

Probe calibration (weekly).

Boil/ice test on the digital probe, recorded against the calibration log. Drift invalidates the readings — calibration is the proof the readings are real.

The failure

What to do when the reading fails.

Step 1 · Record the failure.

Don't retry. Don't "just take another reading". The first read is the real read. Log it as a fail.

Step 2 · Identify the stock.

What was in the cabinet? When was it last known good? Use last-known-good plus time-temp combination to decide discard vs use.

Step 3 · Take corrective action.

Move stock to a working fridge. Discard time-temperature-abused product. Call the engineer. Whatever the action is, it's logged.

Step 4 · Retest and document.

Once the cabinet is working again — new reading, taken at intervals, logged. The audit trail closes the loop on the failure.

Why this matters more than the reading itself.

The EHO knows fridges fail. What separates a 5 from a 3 is whether you spotted, recorded and acted on the failure. The audit trail is the evidence.

FAQ

Fridge temperature logs, asked & answered.

How often should you record fridge temperatures?

Twice a day is the practical UK standard — opening and closing. Busy kitchens add a midday check.

What temperature should a commercial fridge be?

UK law requires ≤8°C; operational target is 0–5°C. Freezers run at -18°C or colder.

What do you do if a fridge reads over temperature?

Record the failed reading, take corrective action (move stock, discard, call engineer), retest. The audit trail of the failure is what the EHO wants.
